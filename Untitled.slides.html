The definition of R(r)¶

Radial distribution function: how many pairs of atoms are there at distance $r$ in a unit volume.

$$\large R(r) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{u} \sum_{v} \frac{f_u f_v}{\bar{f}^2} \delta(r - r_{uv})$$

The integral is the number of atoms in the structure.

$$\large \int_{0}^{\infty} R(r) dr = N$$

Definition of the form factor¶

The scattering power (form factor or scattering length) is the strength of scattering from the individual atom.

The definition of G(r)¶

Pair distribution function: how different is the density of atomic pairs at distance $r$ from the average.

$$\large G(r) = \frac{R(r)}{r} - 4{\pi}\rho_0r \text{, in 3D space}$$$$\large G(r) = \frac{R(r)}{r} - 2{\pi}\rho_0 \text{, in 2D space}$$$$\large G(r) = \frac{R(r)}{r} - \frac{2\rho_0}{r} \text{, in 1D space}$$

The integral is zero.

$$\large \int_{0}^{\infty} G(r) r dr = 0$$

An example: 2D square crystal¶

In a two dimensional universe, the fundamental particle is an atom called "tao zi"(Chinese: ). The "tao zi" has scattering power = 1 unit and bond length = 1 unit.

We have an infinite large two dimensional "tao zi" crystal an example.

Counting pairs¶

Every atom is equivalent so we just need to choose one atom as the origin and count the neigbours.

Every atom has the same scattering factor so the weights are 1.

$$ \large R(r) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{u} \sum_{v} \frac{f_u f_v}{\bar{f}^2} \delta(r - r_{uv}) = \sum_{v} \delta(r - r_{0v}) $$

Uncertainty in positions¶

The atoms randomly deviate from the lattice sites and the deviation follows normal distribution. The peaks change from delta functions to Gaussian functions.

$$\large R(r) = \sum_{v} \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp(-\frac{(r - r_{0v})^2}{2\sigma^2})$$

Modeling of G(r): cost function¶

Optimization Problem:

$$\underset{p_0, p_1, ... , p_n}{\mathrm{argmin}} \frac{1}{2} \chi^2(p_0, p_1, ... , p_n)$$

Calculate cost function from residuals:

$$\chi^2(p_0, p_1, ... , p_n) = \int_0^{\infty} \left[ \frac{G_{obs}(r) - G_{cal}(r, p_0, p_1, ... , p_n)}{\Delta G_{obs}(r)} \right] ^2 dr$$

The $G_{cal}(r)$ is determined by structure models:

$$G_{cal}(r, p_0, p_1, ... , p_n) = F(Structure(p_0, p_1, ... , p_n))$$

Modeling of G(r): visualize $\chi^2$¶

Usually, we don't know $\Delta G_{obs}(r)$ so we assume $\Delta G_{obs}(r) = 1$. Usually, the residuals oscillate around zero. The $\chi^2$ is manifested by the amplitude of oscillation.

A small box model is one lattice or one small super lattice. It is usually constrained by the symmetry. Only a few atoms need to be considered.
